# Social Media Content Business — Cold Call Framework

## The Business Idea

### What It Is
A **local social media content service** targeting blue-collar and service-based small businesses (landscapers, auto shops, oil change shops, roofers, blinds/window covering installers, etc.) in the **Franklin/Spring Hill, Tennessee area**.

The core offering is simple:
- Show up to a client's job site or shop **once a month**
- Spend roughly **one hour** filming and photographing
- Only need **10–15 minutes** of the owner's time for talking/interview segments
- Capture enough **photo and video content** to post across social media for an **entire month**
- Optionally handle all the posting for them

### What Makes It Different
Most small businesses post:
- Static graphics with branding and quotes
- Occasional low-quality phone clips with no audio or bad lighting
- Inconsistently — whenever they have time

This service delivers:
- **High-quality, polished video content**
- **Personality-driven** storytelling (real faces, real voices, real work)
- **Consistent monthly posting** without the business owner lifting a finger
- Content that builds **trust before a customer ever calls**

### The Core Value Proposition
> "People don't just check Google anymore — they go to Instagram and Facebook to see *who* they're working with. When they can see your face, your team, and your actual work, it builds trust before they ever pick up the phone."

### Pricing Strategy
- **First month: Free** — no strings, no charge, no contract
- After results: Framed as **less than half the cost of a part-time social media manager**
- Target price point: **~$1,500/month** (introduced after the free trial builds trust)
  - Standard rate is framed at ~$2,500; discount offered for local/relationship clients

## The Cold Call Approach

### Core Mindset
You are **not selling content**. You are selling:
- **Trust** — customers will believe in this business
- **Visibility** — showing up where people already spend time
- **Ease** — taking a burden completely off their plate

The goal of the first call is **not to close**. It is to get a **next step** (a meeting, a trial, a callback).

> "Most of the time, people are going to end the phone call not remembering every detail — but they like you, they feel good, and they're hanging up with a smile. If you can get that, you're sold."

---

## Call Structure (Segmented — Break It Up with Questions)

### 1. Open Fast and Human
Keep the opener under 10 seconds. Just be warm.

> "Hey Wayne, it's Nathaniel — how are you doing?"
> "I'll keep this super quick."

**Purpose:** Lower their guard. Respect their time immediately.

---

### 2. Establish a Personal or Local Connection
Use something real — a past job, a truck you saw, a shop you drive by.

> "My uncle actually got his car repaired at your shop..."
> "I pass your place every time I go get coffee up in Columbia..."
> "I just wanted to say thank you again — we've been loving the blinds you installed at Messenger."

**Purpose:** This is not a random sales call. You are a neighbor, a familiar face, a community member.

---

### 3. Compliment + Credibility Bridge
Affirm what they're already doing before suggesting anything.

> "I follow your page — I love seeing local businesses doing their thing on social media."
> "You guys do great work."

**Purpose:** Their ego stays intact. You are an upgrade, not a critic.

**Never say:**
- "Your social media isn't good"
- "You're doing this wrong"
- "I think I could do better than what you're doing"

---

### 4. Early Engagement Question — Get a "Yes" Fast
Drop the offer early and simply. Let them respond.

> "What would you think about me coming in and getting you a full month of social media content — completely free?"

**Purpose:** Get them talking. Stack an early small yes. Don't monologue for two minutes before asking anything.

*Pause. Let them respond.*

---

### 5. Preface What You're About to Say (Key Technique)
Before any explanation, **signal what's coming**. This keeps people engaged.

> "Here's how I do it — super simple."
> "A lot of people ask me why this even matters..."
> "So just so you know what to expect..."

**Purpose:** Prefacing prevents people from tuning out. It signals: *this will be short and make sense*.

---

### 6. Describe the Process — Keep It to One or Two Sentences
Only explain the process **after** they've shown interest. Keep it tight.

> "I come out for about an hour, stay totally out of your way, grab footage of your team working, and I only need about 10–15 minutes of your time for some quick talking clips. Everything else I handle behind the scenes."

**Never include in this section:**
- Your editing process
- Your gear or equipment
- Your background story
- Your full content strategy

**Purpose:** Make it feel effortless for them.

---

### 7. Engagement Checkpoint
Ask again. Keep it conversational.

> "How does that sound so far?"
> "Does that make sense?"

**Purpose:** Keep them engaged. You want micro-agreements throughout, not one big yes at the end.

---

### 8. Explain the Value (Frame It First)
Preface it, then say it simply.

> "A lot of people ask me — like, why does this even matter or what does this actually do for me..."
> "What I've found is people don't just check Google anymore — they go to Instagram and Facebook, and they want to see *who* they're working with. When they can see your face, your team, your actual work — it builds trust before they ever call you."

**Purpose:** Sell the *feeling*, not the feature. The feeling is: *customers will trust me and choose me.*

---

### 9. Remove Risk / Reassure
Keep this light. Don't oversell the "free" angle — just state it clearly.

> "I'm not charging you anything for this — I just want to help you out and show you what it could look like."

**Purpose:** Eliminate fear of commitment. No contract. No catch. No pressure.

---

### 10. Soft Close
Simple. Easy. Forward-moving.

> "When's a good time next week I could swing by for about an hour?"

**Purpose:** The ask should feel like a casual next step, not a sales close.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Talking for more than **45–60 seconds** without asking a question
- Saying you're "testing" or "experimenting" or treating them like a "guinea pig"
- Critiquing their current social media strategy
- Over-explaining your process or background before they've shown interest
- Sounding uncertain or apologetic
- Bringing up pricing or "signing something" on the first call
- Asking what their budget is on the first call
- Asking what they're currently doing for marketing (it puts them on the defensive)

---

## The Tone You're Going For

- **Confident**, not salesy
- **Helpful**, not pushy
- **Clear**, not complex

> You're not convincing them of something hard. You're offering something that should feel like an obvious yes.

---

## Ideal Outcome of the First Call

They hang up:
- Liking you
- Feeling good
- Expecting a follow-up text or email with examples

That's it. You haven't sold them anything yet. You've just opened a door.
