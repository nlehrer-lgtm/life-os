# Levi Smith's Cold Call Coaching Notes
### For Nathaniel's Blue-Collar Social Media Content Business

---

## The Big Picture Mindset

The goal of the first call is simple: **leave them smiling**. Most people won't remember every detail of what you said — but if they hang up liking you and feeling good, you've already won. The content, the pitch, the pricing — all of that comes later. The call just opens the door.

> "If you can get that, you're sold."

---

## Levi's Core Principles

### 1. Never Sound Salesy — Ever
You're not pitching. You're helping. Everything you say should feel like *you're giving them more than you're getting.*

- **"Here's my offer"** — sounds transactional
- **"Here's a phenomenal opportunity"** — sounds like you're doing them a favor

The framing matters. You want them thinking: *this guy is getting way less out of this than I am.*

---

### 2. Break Everything Into Segments — Ask Questions

If you talk too long without asking a question, people check out. Full stop.

**Rule of thumb:** Never go more than 45–60 seconds without asking something. Break your explanation into 2 segments minimum, each followed by a question.

> "Does that make sense?" / "How does that sound so far?" / "What do you think?"

These micro-questions do two things:
1. Keep them engaged
2. Stack small yeses before the big ask

---

### 3. The "Only What They See" Rule

> *"If you ever catch yourself talking about yourself or your internal process — hang up."*

This is Levi's hardest line and his most important one. **Clients don't care how you edit, what camera you use, or how your workflow runs.** They care about what *their* experience looks like.

Every description of your process should be filtered through their perspective:

- "I use Premiere Pro and usually spend about 3 hours editing per video..." — wrong
- "You'll barely notice I'm there — I stay out of the way and you'll have a full month of content without it interrupting your day." — right

---

### 4. Shorten the "Why"
The value explanation (why social media matters, why people look businesses up on Instagram, etc.) is important — but it drags if it goes too long. Keep it tight. One or two clear sentences, then move on.

Good tight version:
> "People don't just check Google anymore — they go to Instagram and Facebook, and when they can see your face and your team, they trust you before they ever call."

Then stop. Let them respond. Don't keep explaining.

---

## The Four Questions Every Client Needs Answered

Levi's framework — these are the only things running through their head on the call. Answer them clearly and in order, then close.

| # | Their Question | How to Answer It |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **How much of my time will this take?** | "About 10–15 minutes of your time — that's it." |
| 2 | **What am I actually going to get?** | "A full month of polished photo and video content, ready to post." |
| 3 | **How long until I get it after the shoot?** | [Answer based on your actual turnaround] |
| 4 | **What will this do for me?** | "It builds trust before a customer ever picks up the phone to call you." |

Work through these naturally in conversation — don't recite them. But make sure they all get answered before you go for the soft close.

## Call Structure (Levi's Segmented Approach)

### Segment 1 — Warm Open + Personal Connection + Early Offer

Get in, establish who you are, compliment what they're doing, then drop the offer fast.

> "Hey [Name], it's Nathaniel — I'll keep this super quick. I actually drive by your shop all the time / my uncle got his car repaired there... I follow you guys on social media and I love seeing local businesses doing their thing. I wanted to reach out — what would you think about me coming in and getting you a full month of social media content, completely free?"

*Then stop. Let them answer.*

---

### Engagement Check *(after they respond)*

Even a short response is enough. Affirm it, then move into segment 2.

> "Yeah, totally — and here's what it actually looks like on your end, super simple..."

---

### Segment 2 — The Process (From Their Perspective Only)

Keep this short. Only what they'll experience.

> "I come out for about an hour, stay totally out of your way, grab footage of your team doing what they do. I only need about 10–15 minutes of your time for a few quick talking clips — who you are, what you do, why you do it. Everything else I handle behind the scenes."

*Then ask:*
> "How does that sound?"

---

### Segment 3 — The Value (If They Ask or You Sense Hesitation)

Preface it first, then deliver it tight.

> "A lot of people ask me — like, what does this actually do for me? And here's what I always say: people don't just check Google anymore. They go to Instagram and Facebook, and they want to see who they're working with. When they can see your face, your team, your actual work — it builds trust before they ever call you."

*Then ask:*
> "Does that make sense?"

---

### Soft Close

Make it feel like the obvious next step.

> "When's a good time next week I could swing by for about an hour?"

## Language Swaps — Levi's Edits

| Instead of... | Say this... |
|---|---|
| "Here's my offer" | "Here's a phenomenal opportunity" |
| "I'll come in and film you" | "I'll come out and float behind the scenes" |
| "This is what I do for all my clients" | "Here's what it looks like on your end" |
| Any mention of your editing/post-production | Cut it entirely |
| Pitching the paid tier on the first call | Leave it completely out |

---

## What the First Call Should NOT Do

- Pitch the paid service
- Ask about their budget
- Ask what they're currently doing for marketing
- Explain your gear, editing software, or workflow
- Talk about yourself for more than a sentence
- Leave any silence unfilled with a question

---

## Ideal End State of Call #1

They hang up:
- Liking you
- Feeling like they're the one getting the better end of the deal
- Expecting a follow-up text or a visit

**That's the whole win.** You haven't sold them anything yet. You just made a friend.
