# [[Job Site Social]] — Business Audit & Growth Plan (June 2026)

> Consulting teardown of the current approach: what's broken, what's working, who the real customers are, and the three plays that get this to $75–100K/year. Built from a 15-question discovery session on 2026-06-12. Pairs with `business-spec.md` (the outward story) — this file is the inward truth.

## The Verdict in One Paragraph

[[Job Site Social]] is not failing to gain traction — it has barely applied any force. Twenty dials in two months produced one paying client and one near-miss, which is a *good* conversion rate on a sample too small to mean anything. The real problems: the income goal is mathematically impossible at current prices, the product is sold as unmeasurable "trust" with no proof engine behind it, the free month has no bridge to a paid month, and the only sales channel is the one that hides the business's biggest asset — Nathaniel's face and craft.

## The Math That Doesn't Close

| | Value |
|---|---|
| 12-month income goal | $75–100K/yr → $6,250–8,333/mo |
| Capacity ceiling | 4–5 shoots (= clients)/month |
| **Required revenue per client** | **$1,250–1,667/month, every seat filled** |
| Current realized price | $750 ([[Hushed Lawn Care]]) |
| Stated personal floor | $400 |
| Goal at $750 × 5 clients | $45K/yr — half the goal |
| Goal at $400 floor × 5 | $24K/yr — a quarter of the goal |

Every client seat is scarce (max 5). Every seat sold below ~$1,250 makes the goal unreachable.

**Unit economics at honest costs** (editing ~$200 + captions/posting ~$50 per client/month once [[Levi Smith]]'s editor arrangement is formalized):

- $750 client → ~$500 net ÷ 2.5 hrs → ~$200/hr
- $1,500 client → ~$1,250 net ÷ 2.5 hrs → ~$500/hr
- 5 full-management clients at $1,500 = $7,500/mo gross, ~$6,250/mo net → **$75K/yr, exactly the goal floor.** Hitting $100K means ~$1,900/client or a sixth seat.

## What's Actually Working (Keep It)

- **Funnel conversion is fine.** 20 dials → 15 connects → 10 decision-maker conversations → 2 free shoots → 1 paid. 5% dial-to-paid on cold calls is respectable. The problem is volume, not script. [[Levi Smith]]'s framework works.
- **The delivery stack is real leverage.** Outsourced editing + posting (~$250/client/mo) keeps Nathaniel's time at ~2.5 hrs/client/month. That's an agency margin profile most solo operators never reach.
- **The thesis is validated.** [[Hushed Lawn Care]] renewed for month 2. An owner with no social presence will pay monthly for done-for-you content. Levi's parallel business in Colorado proves it twice.
- **The Hushed negotiation instinct.** [[Zac Musgrove]] countered at $500; the response held $750 and **cut scope (3 posts/wk → 2) instead of cutting price**. That is exactly right. The Melton call was the opposite move — that contrast is the lesson.
- **Research quality.** The 80-prospect scored report is better prep than most funded agencies carry.

## The Gaps — What's Wrong and How to Fix It

### 1. Volume is a rounding error
20 dials in ~2 months is 2.5 per week. Nothing about the model can be judged at this sample size.
**Fix:** 10 dials + 3 in-person drop-ins per week, minimum, logged with next-action dates.

### 2. The goal and the price contradict each other
(See the math table.) Selling at $750 with a $400 floor while needing $1,250+/seat means working toward a goal the pricing has already forfeited.
**Fix:** Two-tier price card, stated flat, never improvised: **$950/mo content-only** (client posts it) / **$1,500/mo full management** (shoot + edit + captions + posting). Absolute floor **$750**. The $400 floor is dead. "Flexible" is retired from the vocabulary.

### 3. Pricing is improvised at the worst possible moment
The [[Melton Auto Styling]] sequence: owner asks the price → hears three numbers in 20 seconds ($2,500 → "half that" → $1,000 + $500) → then *"I can be flexible, whatever would work."* Descending prices signal the number is invented; "flexible" signals it's negotiable to zero. He nodded, thanked, and ghosted.
**Fix:** One number, then silence. "Month-to-month, $1,500 a month, everything done for you. Most owners compare that to a part-time social media hire at three grand." Stop talking. The silence is the close.

### 4. The free month has no bridge to the paid month
"I'd love to bless your company, maybe we'll talk later" is genuine — and it sets up a price ambush at the moment of maximum awkwardness, *after* the free work is delivered. [[Melton Auto Styling]] died exactly there. STATUS already flagged the other half: no budget qualification *before* the shoot.
**Fix:** Name the number before the free shoot: *"Month one is on me, no strings. If you love it, month two is $1,500 and I keep it rolling — fair?"* A "fair" before the shoot is a soft contract. Anyone who balks just saved a free shoot for a prospect who won't.

### 5. The product is sold as unmeasurable
"I build trust, not followers" is a real wedge — but as currently sold there is nothing to point to at renewal time. No baseline was captured on [[Hushed Lawn Care]] month 1 (STATUS flagged it; still not done). When Zac asks what $9K/year bought him, there is currently no answer.
**Fix:** Instrument trust. Track monthly: profile visits, website taps from social, DMs, Google Business photo views and calls, and have the client ask "how'd you hear about us?" Screenshot a before/after dashboard every month. Trust becomes a number.

### 6. The business fails its own audit
The pitch is "customers check your socials before they call you." [[Job Site Social]] has no live website, no Instagram of its own, and prospects get a Google Drive folder plus a Colorado account ([[Levi Smith]]'s). A prospect who Googles mid-call finds nothing.
**Fix:** Ship the half-built Next.js site as one page. Launch @jobsitesocial with the Hushed work and behind-the-scenes of every shoot. The making-of content *is* the prospecting content.

### 7. One channel — and it's the weakest one for this product
Cold phone hides the face, the gear, the work: everything that closes. Zero referral asks ([[Zac Musgrove]] has never been asked, despite [[Levi Smith]] calling referrals priceless). Zero in-person selling. 2–4 of 20 dials died at gatekeepers — gatekeepers don't exist when you walk in with a camera and a card.
**Fix:** Referral ask this week. In-person drop-ins on the 9–10/10 prospect list. Supplier counters (SiteOne, Winsupply, Ferguson), chamber, local trade Facebook groups.

### 8. Folding on the #1 objection
Half of dead calls end at "we already do social media," answered with pure positivity and a goodbye — while pre-call research shows most of those pages are dead since 2019 or iPhone-grade. A mostly-false objection is being taken at face value.
**Fix:** *"Love it — who's shooting your video?"* Then: *"Totally fair — quick honest question, when's the last time something got posted?"* Then the free side-by-side: one shoot, compare it to what they're running.

### 9. No follow-up system
8 of 10 decision-maker conversations went nowhere and are untracked. Service deals close on touches 3–7; the current model is one touch and done (except Melton, who got the full-court press *after* the pricing fumble instead of a clean setup before it).
**Fix:** Every call gets a status, a next action, and a date in the prospects folder. Quarterly re-touch on every "no." Seasonal re-entry: roofers around storm season, HVAC before summer.

### 10. Concentration and dependency risk
One client = 100% of revenue. The editing/posting stack is an informal favor from [[Levi Smith]]'s team.
**Fix:** Formalize editor pricing now (~$250/client/mo) so COGS is real before scaling; get to 3 clients fast so no single renewal is existential.

## Top Clientele — Who This Serves Best in 2026

Why this list works *right now*: Spring Hill / Williamson County is one of the fastest-growing areas in Tennessee. Thousands of new residents arrive every year with **no word-of-mouth network** — they hire whoever looks trustworthy on Instagram, Facebook, Nextdoor, and Google. Meanwhile feeds are flooding with AI-generated slop, which makes *real faces doing real work* the scarcest, most differentiated content there is. That is exactly what [[Job Site Social]] sells.

### Tier 1 — High-ticket home trades · charge $1,500–2,500/mo
**Roofing · HVAC · Plumbing (service + install) · Electrical**
- Tickets of $8K–30K: one closed job pays for a year of content. The ROI sentence writes itself.
- Massive trust deficit (storm chasers and bad actors poisoned these categories) → trust content has its highest value here.
- They already spend on marketing (Google LSA, Angi) and resent it — a flat $1,500 for content they own is an easy comparison.
- Already researched and scored: Newsom Roofing, Five Points Roofing, Tansil Heating and Cooling, Heritage Heating & Cooling, C&M Heating & Cooling, Kennewick Electric, Delta South Electric.

### Tier 2 — Transformation trades · charge $1,500–2,000/mo
**Remodelers · kitchen & bath · epoxy/concrete coatings · decks & outdoor living · pools**
- Before/after is the highest-performing genre of local content; the work sells itself on camera.
- Williamson County new-build money; long consideration cycles where social proof decides the winner.
- Already scored: Music City Epoxy, My Garage Floor Guys, Guardian Garage Floors.

### Tier 3 — Premium recurring home services · charge $750–1,000/mo
**Lawn care · pressure washing · detailing · pest control**
- Proven fit ([[Hushed Lawn Care]]); recurring-revenue owners understand paying monthly for monthly value.
- Smaller budgets — the volume and case-study tier, not the path to $100K on its own.

### Tier 4 — Auto repair & body shops · charge $750–1,250/mo
- Great content potential, but mixed budget culture — both pipeline deaths so far ([[Melton Auto Styling]], [[Beechcroft Automotive]]) were automotive. Qualify revenue hard before any free shoot. Insurance-fed body shops over general repair.

### Tier 5 — Selective adjacents · charge $1,000–1,500/mo
**Vet clinics · gun shops with retail/training · outdoor power equipment dealers** (wave-2 research categories)
- Real budgets, trust-driven purchases; slightly off the "job site" brand. Take opportunistically, don't chase.

**The profile inside every tier:** single location, owner-operated, $1M+ revenue, owner ~30–55 (or second-generation taking over — they already believe in social), already spending *something* on marketing, dead-or-DIY social presence.

## Three Growth Plays

### Play A — The Proof Engine
Make the business its own best advertisement. Instrument [[Hushed Lawn Care]] into a real numbers case study, ship the one-page site, launch the [[Job Site Social]] Instagram, post behind-the-scenes of every shoot. Replace blind cold calls with personalized 60-second video audits of the prospect's current presence, texted or emailed to the owner. Sell the way the product works: show, don't tell.

### Play B — Up-Market Repricing (the Anchor-Trade Strategy)
Stop selling $750 content to businesses that count dollars; sell a $1,500–2,500 trust system to trades where one job is worth $8–30K. Lead with ROI framing: *"If this closes one extra roof a year, it paid for itself ten times."* Two-tier price card, price named before the free shoot, no flexibility language. Five Tier-1 clients at $1,500–1,800 = the entire income goal, inside the capacity ceiling.

### Play C — The Referral Flywheel
Quit being a stranger. Ask [[Zac Musgrove]] for three intros this week (incentive: a free month per converted referral). Walk into the 9–10/10 prospects with a card and a 60-second reel. Be physically present where owners gather: supplier counters, chamber, trade breakfasts, local Facebook groups. One free "local legend" spotlight reel a month on a beloved local business builds the brand publicly. Become the guy who films Williamson County's tradesmen.
