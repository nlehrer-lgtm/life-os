## 🧰 On Deck #1 — The "Meeting the Needs of Your Leader" Conversation

_Material to pull from when his question lands. Anchor in his own words, not yours._

### Open with this meta-frame:

> **"The shortest answer is: bring me decisions, not problems. You already have the discernment — I just need you firing it *up* the chain at me, not only *down* the chain at your work."**

That sentence does three things at once:
1. Names his **Discernment** genius — he already has the muscle.
2. Reframes "meeting my needs" as **applying what he already does well, in a new direction.** Not a new skill — a new vector.
3. Sidesteps his younger-sibling / overstepping wariness. He's not taking over — he's *aiming the same instinct at a new target.*

### Then name his own 5 leadership ideas back to him:

He already wrote these in Asana — pull them up as the **foundation**, not as you adding new ideas:

1. Leading by example
2. Looking for ways to make the jobs of his leader and team easier
3. Being more vocal with encouragement
4. Getting better at giving feedback well
5. Stronger communication with other team leads

**Say:**
> "These are yours. You wrote them. I want to anchor this whole conversation in *your* words, not mine. Of these five — which one already feels natural to you? And which one feels like the stretch?"

### Then offer 2 concrete examples (save the 3rd for next time):

**Example 1 — "Bring me a recommendation, not a question."** _Ties to his #2._
> "When you hit a fork on [[MCUP]] opener — two casting directions, two ways to build Lisa's set — don't ask me 'which one?' Send me three lines: *'Here are the two paths. I'm leaning A because X. Need a yes or a redirect by EOD.'* That moves my brain from doing the thinking to confirming the thinking. You just gave me back 20 minutes."

> *Why this lands for him: his Discernment genius IS the recommendation engine. You're not asking him to invent a new skill — you're asking him to let you see the output of the skill he already runs.*

**Example 2 — "Tell me what's stuck on Tuesday, not Friday."** _Ties to his #4 + his current frustration._
> "Lisa's set is stalled — your agenda says so. The fact that I'm finding that out in a Wednesday prep doc instead of a Tuesday text is the gap. **Stalled work surfaced early is a gift to me. Stalled work surfaced late is a fire.** Same information, totally different cost."

**Example 3 (hold in reserve) — "Run the first pass like I'm not going to review it."** _Ties to his #1._
> "When a storyboard or asset comes to me, I should be reviewing for *strategic alignment* — not catching basic issues you would've caught with one more pass. If you ship at 90%, I'm doing the last 10% in my head and it costs me real cycles. If you ship at 99%, my job is just to bless it and unblock you. That's the trust loop."

### Bridge his feedback to this conversation:

> "Your question and your feedback to me are actually the same question. You're asking for a better map of the field so you can run ahead on it. You can't anticipate what you can't see. Let's solve that together — that's a thing we co-design."

### [[Working Genius]] guardrail:
- His **Wonder** frustration means *open-ended* "look 6 months down the road" questions tire him out.
- The "anticipation" muscle works best for him when it's **specific and concrete** — *"what's the next decision on [[MCUP]] opener you'll need me to make?"* not *"what's coming this fall?"*
- Keep his anticipation lane **tactical**, not visionary-vague.
