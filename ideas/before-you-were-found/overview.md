# Before You Were Found — Idea Overview

> An allegorical adventure — in the spirit of [[Faith]] and the company of *The Great Divorce*, *Narnia*, and *The Alchemist* — about a man who wakes in a strange field and spends the whole story striving to escape, only to learn that the oasis he woke beside was the destination all along. The point of the whole journey is to be *seen* by the source of life, and to stop running long enough to receive it.

**Type:** Book (could branch into film, testimony, or song)
**Status:** Concept + first full prose draft written
**Pillars:** [[Faith]], [[Joy & Rest]]
**Origin:** Sparked in Nathaniel's prayer closet — a vision that may be a story (2026)

---

## The Core Truth

A man cannot defeat his besetting sin by *striving* against it. He defeats it by living near the source of life — a relationship with God that is **passionate, intimate, and committed all at once** (not just one of the three). You don't beat sin by chasing the cure; you beat it by falling more in love with God than with the sin. "It all comes down to loving God more than you love your sin."

The whole adventure is a long way of arriving at a very simple truth — the *Alchemist* move.

## The Spine of the Story

A man — **Nate** (standing in for Nathaniel) — wakes in an endless field beside a small oasis: a bubbling spring, a fig tree, figs to eat, shade, safety. He has lost his memory and, slowly, remembers he has lost his family to a **sexual sin** he fed in secret for years.

He spends the story trying to *escape*:
- Walking to every edge of the world — each one drops off into nothingness.
- Battling a **red glass wall** with a rock until he breaks through — only to find the same field on the other side, and the wall sealed behind him.
- Mapping, digging, climbing, counting — the field erases his marks and returns him to the oasis every morning.

Two encounters turn him:
- He sees a **younger version of himself** asleep under the tree — who he was *before* the sin took root.
- He kneels at a **dark pool** and sees a distorted, hardened reflection — what sin does to a man on the inside until the outside follows.

Finally he stops fighting. He lies under the fig tree (**awake — this matters**), and instead of breaking the wall he simply *looks* at the spring. The field changes — not because anything in it changed, but because he did. A presence he'd sensed all along rises and tells him: *I was here before you knew to look. I saw you before you were lost.*

He closes his eyes — and opens them in a hospital chair. He has been in a **coma for ~5 years** after a tragic accident. The field was his imagination. Family photos line the wall; caretakers and his family have kept him alive and prayed over him the whole time. His wife **Mara** has found her footing and moved on (remarried); his son **Eli**, now nine, takes his barely-moving hand and says, *"I prayed for you. Every night."* There is redemption, not rage — a mutual understanding that both were changed in the years he was gone.

**Coda:** He is not healed all at once — body and soul aren't like that. But he keeps the moment under the tree "like a stone in his pocket… as ballast." When the old pull returns (and it does), he returns not to the field but to the *posture*: eyes up, hands open, facing the source instead of running from the dark. "This was not a method. It was a direction."

## The Three Theological Throughlines

1. **The bronze serpent** (Numbers 21 / John 3) — healing comes by *looking*, not striving. Moses lifts the snake; the people only have to look to be healed. Foreshadows Christ lifted up. We are all bitten by sin; the only remedy is to look toward God.
2. **The fig tree** (John 1:47–51) — *being seen*. "Nathanael" means *gift of God*. Jesus says "I saw you under the fig tree" — naming a private, intimate moment Nathanael had forgotten, which is what proved Jesus was the Son of God. The oasis is not *discovered*; it is **remembered**. God was with him under the tree long before He said so.
3. **Jacob's ladder** (John 1:51, Genesis 28) — God meets us in the wilderness; "you will see greater things."

Supporting motifs woven throughout:
- **Sin as far as the east is from the west** (Psalm 103) — the field's east/west wandering.
- **The mirror** (James 1:23–24) — the man who looks at his face and immediately forgets; the distorted reflections in glass and water until, at the end, a clear picture.
- **New creation / put off the old self** (2 Cor 5:17, Ephesians 4) — "the old is gone."
- **Lose your life to save it** (Matthew 16) — the original spark: a man alone in a field, having lost everything.
- **David & Bathsheba / Nathan's ewe-lamb parable** (2 Samuel 11–12) — the cost of hidden sin on a family, and the death it brings; an idea to draw from.
- **Light & shadow** — dimensions, the speed/purpose of light, how light requires shadow, darkness vs. coming into the light. A planned thematic layer (C.S. Lewis–style adventure with these "physical" representations of invisible truths).

## The Big Emotional Aim

A reader should close the book feeling **hope, strength, and passion to beat the sin in their own life** — and understand that the way you overcome is by living every day near the source of life, in a relationship with God that is passionate, intimate, and committed.
